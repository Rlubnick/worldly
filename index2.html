    China

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

    • Acceptable business attire include dresses, pantsuits and light makeup. Dangling and gaudy jewelry are not acceptable.
    • China is a difficult place for anyone to conduct business, however, it may be more difficult for a woman in the field.

    Greetings

    • It is considered polite to give your full name, job position, and location you work at, especially on more formal occasions. Sharing just your full name should suffice in an informal setting.
    • There is a common hierarchy that is generally followed when giving introductions such as: youngest to eldest first, men to women first, inferior to superior first, and host to guest first.

    Social Condtions

    • It is normal for a woman to move in with her husband and his parents after marriage.

    Attire

    • Foreigners going to China for business and vacation are recommended to avoid dressing in low-cut tops, excessive jewelry, and clothing that reveal bare backs.

    Comments

    Thailand

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

    • Business in Thailand is male-dominated, but many traditional sex barriers are disappearing. Recently, the amount of women in executive positions in the workforce are increasing.

    Greetings

    • When in Thailand, it is helpful to know the traditional greetings. A man would say “Sawatdee-krap." A women would say “Sawatdee-kah.” These are used as gender identifiers and women say “ka” after every sentence.

    Social Conditions

    • Women aren’t allowed to enter bots within wats (temple). These are special parts of the temple where only males can enter.
    • Traditional Thai people are strict when it comes to physical contact in public.
    • Women aren’t allowed to touch monks, hand things to monks or to have their feet pointing in the same direction as a monk.
    • Tampons are hard to find in Thai stores.

    Attire

    • The common dress calls for the covering of shoulders and knees (for all genders) and ideally, if possible, ankles as well.

    Comments

    Japan

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

    • In Japanese business people, foreigners and Westerners are typically treated respectfully.
    • In Japanese business people, foreigners and Westerners are typically treated respectfully.
    • Exchanging business cards holds extreme importance, so when offering a business card, hold it with both hands and extend it to the other person. it should be exchanged with the most important person at the meeting first.

    Greetings

    • A formal Japanese greeting typically has little to no physical interaction, largely because Japanese traditions value space and privacy.
    • It is normal to bow, typically with the hands together near the hips or thighs lasting for about two to five seconds. The more respect you want to express, the deeper the bow should be.

    Social Conditions

    • Personal relationships are typically hierarchical with the eldest, men, and senior executives having higher statuses than the youngest, women, and juniors.

    Attire

    • The traditional Japanese attire consists of the kimono. The younger generations prefer to wear more loose-fitting T-shirts and comfortable jeans.

    Comments

    India

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

    • The business industry in India is male-dominated. Aa woman establishing authority during first impressions will increase acceptance among male co-workers.
    • A division in the workplace can be considered normal because of India’s current patriarchal state.
    • Women may not be included in social events or conversation. A business lunch initiated by another woman is acceptable.

    Greetings

    • When meeting a traditional Indian man, females would typically not expect handshakes. It is considered normal to accept a handshake from a more Westernized Indian man.

    Social Conditions

    • Staring, groping, stalking and rape are a frequent occurrence, especially for women.

    Attire

    • Dressing conservatively and respectfully is vital for both acceptance and safety within India, due to the high possibility of being harassed in public. Women are expected to cover their shoulders, legs and cleavage.

    Comments

    South Korea

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

    • Foreign women may have difficulty doing business in Korea. Although women are becoming more accepted in Korean businesses, Korean men generally prefer to negotiate with men.
    • Foreign businesswomen should always act elegant, refined and very "feminine.” Laughing and loud talking are frowned upon.

    Greetings

    • Korean women usually nod slightly and will not shake hands with Western men. Western women may offer their hand to a Korean man.
    • It is considered impolite to pour your own drink or to pour another woman’s drink.
    • Korean women seldom shake hands. A Western woman can offer her hand to a Korean man, but should not to a Korean woman.

    Social Conditions

    • Do not pour your own drink, but offer to pour for others. It is common to trade and fill each other's cup. To refuse is an insult. Women pour men's drinks, but never another woman's drink. A woman may pour her own drink. Leave some drink in your glass if you don't want a refill.

    Attire

    • Women dress modestly. Prepare to sit on the floor; avoid straight, tight skirts.

    Comments

    Hong Kong

    info | comments

    Information

    Business

  • It is typical business dress code for women to dress conservatively in suits, dresses, or skirts and blouses.
  • Business typically runs smoothly for foreign businesswomen in Hong Kong.

    • Greetings

    • It is common for everyone to shake hands with each other upon meeting and departing. The common handshake is not as firm as a Western one.
    • It is considered polite to let others know about one's health or current activities upon greeting.

    Social Conditions

    • Although it is not common for Chinese women to consume alcohol, it is more accepted for Western women to drink alcohol, but in moderation.

    Attire

    • There is a common hierarchy when it comes to introductions in Hong Kong:
      • In a professional setting, the higher ranked person is introduced first, and the lower-ranked are then introduced. Elders’ introductions come before that of younger persons and women before men.
      • In the family setting, family members are introduced in order of the eldest first and the youngest last.
